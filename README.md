# BMI and Labor Market Earnings: A Gender-Based Analysis
This project investigates whether Body Mass Index (BMI) affects individual earnings and whether this effect differs by gender, using data from the National Longitudinal Survey of Youth 1997 (NLSY97).
# Overview
Using a sample of 3,684 American individuals, we build a series of OLS regression models to estimate the relationship between BMI categories (underweight, healthy, overweight, obese) and hourly wages, controlling for human capital factors, demographic characteristics, and social variables.
# Key Findings

Obese individuals earn significantly less than healthy-weight peers, even after controlling for education, tenure, and demographics
The wage penalty is strongly gendered: obese women earn ~21% less than healthy-weight women, compared to ~2.5% for men
Results are consistent with Becker's taste-based discrimination theory

# Methods & Tools

Language: R
Models: 7 progressively specified OLS regression models
Techniques: Feature engineering, dummy variables, interaction terms, outlier removal, descriptive statistics
Data: NLSY97
